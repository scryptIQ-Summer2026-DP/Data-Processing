    Introduction

    When working with real-world datasets, the variables you measure rarely exist in isolation. Typically two (or more) features may rise and fall together as they are intangibly linked, something very common in biological systems that are fully integrated. Understanding these feature relationships is a foundational step in any data analysis pipeline, and an essential one before building machine learning (ML) models.

    Scatter plots

    The most common way to quantify a relationship between two variables is to measure how consistently they vary together. When one increases and the other tends to as well, they are positively related. When one increases as the other decreases, they are negatively related. When there is no consistent pattern, they are uncorrelated. This matters in ML for a practical reason: if two features are measuring essentially the same thing, they provide redundant information. Including both gives that signal disproportionate weight during training, potentially at the expense of other, more independent features. Identifying and addressing this kind of redundancy is a key part of feature selection.

    In this section we will look at the main tools for exploring feature relationships: scatter plots for visual inspection, the Pearson correlation coefficient and covariance for quantification, and heatmaps for surveying relationships across many features at once.

    The Correlation Coefficient

    The Pearson correlation coefficient ($r$) quantifies the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two variables, always producing a value between −1 and +1.

    • r = +1: a perfect positive linear relationship — as one variable increases, the other increases proportionally. All points in a scatter plot lie exactly on a straight line with a positive slope.

    • r = −1: a perfect negative linear relationship — as one increases, the other decreases proportionally. All points lie on a straight line with a negative slope.

    • r = 0: no linear relationship. The positive and negative contributions cancel out exactly.

    All real-world correlations fall somewhere in between. It is worth noting that $r = 0$ does not necessarily mean the two variables are unrelated, only that there is no linear pattern. A strong curved relationship, for instance, can still produce an $r$ close to zero.

    Finally, $r$ is a symmetric measure: the correlation of X with Y is identical to the correlation of Y with X. There is no implied direction of influence between the two variables.

    Covariance

    Covariance is the raw, unnormalised form of correlation. Whereas correlation is bounded between −1 and +1, covariance can be any real number, with its magnitude depending entirely on the scale of the two variables.

    Variance

    Covariance is also an extension of the variance metric, the measure of how much data points in a single feature vary across the population:

    $$\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu)^2$$

    where $x$ is the data and $\mu$ is the mean of the data.

    From this equation, you can see that variance is simply the average of how far every value deviates from the population mean $(x_i - \mu)$. The reason it is squared is to over-represent large deviations (outliers) in the final metric, and because a squared term is mathematically preferable to work with for many algorithms.

    Covariance

    Covariance applies the idea of variance to two variables, not one. Finding the differences of one feature (x) from its mean and multiplying them by the differences of another feature (y) to their mean:

    $$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu_x)(y_i - \mu_y)$$

    A positive covariance means the variables tend to move together; negative means they move in opposite directions. A problem with covariance is interpretability: a covariance of 0.05 between two variables measured in milligrams is not directly comparable to a covariance of 0.05 between variables measured in kilograms.

    The Pearson coefficient resolves this by dividing the covariance by the multiplication of the two standard deviations, effectively normalising it to the [−1, 1] scale:

    $$r = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \cdot \sigma_Y}$$

    In practice, you will almost always report correlation rather than covariance for interpretation. However, covariance is important internally for a lot of machine learning tools, such as principal component analysis (PCA), which you will encounter in later modules.

    Heatmaps

    With 77 protein columns in this dataset, inspecting correlations pair by pair is impractical. The correlation matrix we have seen computed will return a Pearson coefficient between every pair of columns, where the diagonal is always 1 (every variable is perfectly correlated with itself).

    A better way to visualise everything together is a heatmap where the coefficient values are encoded with colours, making structural patterns immediately visible. Clusters of co-regulated proteins appear as bright blocks; uncorrelated or inversely related pairs appear in contrasting colours.

    To plot the full correlation matrix use the method ax.imshow() which renders any input data as a colour image, effectively converting the number to a colour given a scale. To ax.imshow() you pass:

    • The values from a corrlation matrix in the form of a NumPy array

    • The colour scale: here we have used RdBu_r which is Red to Blue - see here for the full list

    • The minimum and maximum values in the numerical range (vmin and vmax). This is important as the colour bar will scale to the data, giving you false ideas of absolute difference where the range is small.

    The labels on the x- & y-axis are the same given it is symmetrical, therefore you should pass the same list of columns to set_xticks and 'set_yticks'.

    Lastly, you should always include a colour bar by adding it to the plot with plt.colourbar(). To this we pass:

    • The plot itself, here saved to the variable im

    • The axes to plot to with ax

    • It's named label with label

    • It's size with fraction

    • How far it is from the plot with pad

    Finding highly correlated pairs raises two important practical questions.

    What do we do with redundant features? In machine learning, two features with a correlation above roughly 0.9 carry nearly identical information. Including both does not add much to a model and can inflate feature importance scores or slow training. A common pre-processing step is to remove one column from each highly-correlated pair, retaining the one with a stronger relationship to your target variable.

    A final caveat: Pearson only captures linear relationships and is sensitive to outliers and skew. Whilst it will work on non-normal distributed data, some implementations of Pearson's will perform a statistical p-value test that the two samples are uncorrelated with the assumption they are normally distributed. If you want to use this or your data is extremely non-normal then a rank-based alternative such as Spearman's rank correlation is more robust, as it correlates the ranks of the values rather than the raw values themselves.

    Summary & Quiz

    In this section we quantified how features move together. Scatter plots gave a visual read, and colouring by category revealed subgroups that a single global statistic can hide. We introduced the Pearson correlation coefficient — a normalised, symmetric measure of linear association bounded between −1 and +1 — alongside covariance, its unbounded, scale-dependent basis. Heatmaps then let us survey the whole correlation matrix at once, highlighting blocks of co-regulated proteins and redundant feature pairs.
